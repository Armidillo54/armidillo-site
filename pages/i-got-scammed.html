I Just Got Scammed.
What Do I Do Right Now?

Take a breath. You're not alone — the FTC received 6.5 million fraud reports in 2024. The next 24 hours matter most. Follow these steps in order, right now.

How did you pay the scammer? Jump to specific guidance:

1

Stop All Contact With the Scammer — Right Now

Block the scammer on every channel immediately — phone, email, social media, WhatsApp, everything. Do not respond to any further messages, no matter what they say.

⚠️ Critical: Do not send any more money

Scammers will call back with a "recovery" offer — promising they can get your money back if you pay them again. This is always a second scam. Hang up immediately.

  • Take screenshots of every conversation, payment receipt, and profile before blocking
  • Save the scammer's phone number, email address, and any usernames
  • Write down the dates, amounts, and exact sequence of events while it's fresh
2

Contact Your Bank or Payment Service — This Hour

Call the fraud line immediately. Time is critical — banks can sometimes reverse or recall transactions within hours, but not days.

💳 Credit Card or Debit Card

Call the number on the back of your card right now. Say: "I need to report a fraudulent charge and dispute it." Credit cards have the strongest chargeback protection — dispute the charge under "goods/services not received" or "fraud."

  • Credit cards: up to 60 days to dispute, strong chargeback rights under the Fair Credit Billing Act
  • Debit cards: report within 2 days for maximum protection (limits liability to $50)
  • Ask the bank to put a fraud alert on your account and issue a new card number

🏦 Bank Transfer or Wire Transfer

Call your bank's fraud line immediately. Ask them to recall or reverse the wire. Some banks can cancel a wire if it hasn't been picked up yet. Success rate drops sharply after the first few hours.

  • US: Also contact the receiving bank's fraud department if you know it
  • Canada: Contact your bank AND report to the CAFC within 24 hours
  • File a report with your local police — banks often require a police report to process fraud claims

📱 Zelle, Venmo, Cash App, PayPal

These are the hardest to recover. Peer-to-peer payment apps generally don't offer buyer protection for payments you authorized. But you must still report it:

  • Zelle: Report within the app and call your bank — Zelle is bank-backed, your bank has more leverage. Say you were "deceived into authorizing" the payment
  • Venmo/PayPal: Open a dispute immediately in the app. PayPal offers some buyer protection if marked as "goods and services"
  • Cash App: Tap the transaction → "..." → Report and request a refund. Contact support. Cash App fraud rarely results in recovery

₿ Cryptocurrency

Crypto transactions are irreversible by design. There is no "undo" button. Be very cautious of any company promising to recover your crypto — most are secondary scams targeting victims who are already desperate.

  • Contact the exchange you used (Coinbase, Binance, etc.) and report fraud — they may be able to flag the receiving wallet
  • Report to the IC3 (FBI) — they have a crypto fraud unit and have recovered funds in large cases
  • Read our full guide: Lost Crypto to a Scam? Here's What You Can Actually Do

🎁 Gift Cards

Act immediately — gift card fraud can sometimes be stopped if the card hasn't been redeemed yet.

  • Keep the gift card and its packaging — you need the card number and PIN
  • Call the gift card issuer's fraud line (number on the back or on the brand's website)
  • Report to the FTC at ReportFraud.ftc.gov
