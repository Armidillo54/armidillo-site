Cybersecurity Glossary

Plain-English definitions for the terms you encounter in fraud reports, security news, and identity theft stories — written for real people, not IT professionals.

Click any term to expand its definition.

Gift Card Scam
A fraud where the victim is instructed to purchase gift cards and provide the card numbers as payment. No legitimate government agency, utility company, or employer will ever ask for payment via gift card. This is always a scam.
Grandparent Scam
A scam targeting seniors where a fraudster calls pretending to be a grandchild in distress (arrested, in an accident, in hospital) and urgently requests money — often in cash or via wire transfer. Always hang up and call your grandchild directly on a number you know.
Phishing
An attempt to trick you into revealing sensitive information (passwords, credit card numbers, SSN) by pretending to be a trusted entity via email, text, or phone call. Look for mismatched email domains, urgency, and requests for personal information.
Smishing
SMS phishing — fraudulent text messages that link to fake websites or ask you to call a scam number. Often impersonates banks, the IRS, delivery companies, or government agencies.
Vishing
Voice phishing — phone calls designed to extract personal or financial information. Scammers often spoof legitimate phone numbers using VoIP technology.
Identity Theft
When someone uses your personal information (name, SSN, credit card number) without your permission, typically to commit fraud or steal money. Warning signs include unexpected credit denials, unfamiliar accounts on credit reports, and debt collection calls for accounts you didn't open.
Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)
A security process requiring two forms of identification before accessing an account — typically a password plus a code sent to your phone or generated by an authenticator app. Always use 2FA when available, preferably with an authenticator app rather than SMS.
Auto Dialer
Automated telephone equipment that dials numbers sequentially or randomly. Used legitimately by businesses for appointment reminders, but also heavily used by scammers for robocall fraud campaigns.
VoIP Phone Number
A phone number that routes through the internet rather than traditional telephone lines. VoIP numbers are cheap, disposable, and easy to spoof — this is why scammers can appear to call from any number they choose, including government agency numbers.
Man-in-the-Middle Attack
A cyberattack where the attacker secretly intercepts and potentially alters communications between two parties. Common on unsecured public Wi-Fi. This is why you should never access banking or crypto accounts on public networks without a VPN.
Zero-Day Vulnerability
A software security flaw that is unknown to the vendor and for which no patch exists. Called "zero-day" because developers have had zero days to fix it. These are valuable to hackers and intelligence agencies.
Pig Butchering Scam
A sophisticated, long-running fraud combining romance scam tactics with fake crypto investment platforms. The scammer builds trust over weeks or months, introduces a crypto "investment opportunity," helps the victim "make profits" on a fake platform, then disappears when the victim tries to withdraw — taking everything.
Credit Freeze
A free security measure you can place with all three credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) that prevents new credit accounts from being opened in your name. Does not affect existing accounts or your credit score. Highly recommended for identity theft victims and proactive protection.
Dark Web
The portion of the internet not indexed by standard search engines, accessible only via special software (Tor). It's a marketplace for stolen personal data, login credentials, and financial information. Identity protection services like Aura monitor the dark web for your information.
Data Breach
A security incident where unauthorized individuals access, copy, or steal sensitive data. Major breaches have exposed billions of login credentials, SSNs, and financial records. If your email has been in a breach, check haveibeenpwned.com.
Synthetic Identity Fraud
Fraud where criminals combine real identifying information (like a stolen Social Security Number) with fabricated information (fake name, address) to create a new identity. Often targets children's SSNs because the theft goes undetected for years.
Business Email Compromise (BEC)
A sophisticated fraud targeting businesses. Attackers impersonate executives or partners via email to trick employees into making wire transfers or revealing sensitive information. The FBI reports BEC causes billions in losses annually.
Skimming
The illegal capture of credit or debit card data using a device placed over legitimate card readers (ATMs, gas pumps, POS terminals). Skimmers are often difficult to spot — always wiggle card readers before use and cover the keypad when entering PINs.
SIM Swapping
A fraud where attackers convince your mobile carrier to transfer your phone number to a SIM card they control. Once successful, they intercept your SMS verification codes and can access email, banking, and crypto accounts. Use authenticator apps instead of SMS for 2FA.
Credential Stuffing
An automated attack where hackers use lists of stolen username/password combinations (from data breaches) to try logging into many websites simultaneously. This works because many people reuse passwords across sites. A password manager solving this by generating unique passwords for every account.
